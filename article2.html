Sleep and Health: Tips and Strategies for Better Sleep

Varich Journal • December 27, 2023

Turkey contains protein and several vitamins and minerals, such as riboflavin and phosphorus. It is also rich in the amino acid tryptophan, which promotes the production of melatonin. Fatty fish such as salmon, tuna, trout and mackerel are extremely beneficial due to their vitamin D content and omega-3 fatty acids, particularly EPA and DHA. The combination of omega-3 fatty acids and vitamin D in oily fish can have a positive effect on sleep quality, as both have been shown to promote serotonin production. Eating a few grams of turkey or fatty fish before bed can help you fall asleep faster and sleep more deeply, including fewer awakenings during the night.

Circadian rhythms are natural biological fluctuations that occur roughly every 24 hours and regulate various physiological processes in the body. One of the main circadian rhythms is the sleep-wake cycle, which is controlled by an internal clock. These rhythms synchronize with the external environment, ensuring an optimal balance of activity and rest throughout the day, and they are essential for maintaining health and good-quality sleep.

Sleep stages are different phases characterized by varying depths and levels of activity. Sleep is typically divided into four stages: the falling asleep stage, which is the transition from wakefulness to sleep and lasts a few minutes; the light sleep stage, during which consciousness decreases and lasts about 20 minutes; the deep sleep stage, which is hard to wake from and important for physical recovery, lasting over 30 minutes; and the REM stage (rapid eye movement), the dreaming phase, which is vital for mental recovery and occurs several times a night, with increasing duration. The body goes through all these stages approximately 4 to 6 times per night, with each cycle lasting 90 to 110 minutes. A full night of sleep usually consists of several such cycles, totaling around 7 to 9 hours.

Melatonin is a hormone that regulates circadian rhythms and helps control sleep and wake cycles. Its level increases in darkness and decreases in light, signaling the body that it’s time to sleep. Melatonin plays an important role in dream regulation and affects sleep quality and the body’s recovery processes.

In this article, the tips are divided into several categories. Sleep schedule optimization involves having a regular routine for healthy circadian rhythms and better sleep. Relaxation before sleep includes techniques for calming the mind and making it easier to fall asleep. Nutrition and supplements refer to how food and supplements affect sleep quality. Sleep environment organization means creating a supportive space, taking into account lighting and setup.

Sleep Schedule Optimization:

  1. Reduce blue light exposure in the evening. While light exposure during the day is beneficial, at night it has the opposite effect. This is because it influences the circadian rhythm, tricking the brain into thinking it’s still daytime. As a result, it lowers the levels of hormones like melatonin, which help you relax and enter deep sleep. Blue light is mainly emitted in large amounts by lamps and electronic devices such as smartphones and computers, making them the worst offenders in this regard. There are several popular methods to reduce blue light exposure at night. These include using dim red lighting as night illumination, which is less likely to shift the circadian rhythm and suppress melatonin; wearing glasses that block blue light; stopping screen use two to three hours before bed; or installing apps like f.lux to block blue light on your laptop or computer, as well as blue light filter apps for your smartphone.
  2. Reduce irregular or prolonged daytime naps. Short naps lasting 30 minutes or less can be beneficial, but long or irregular naps may negatively affect your sleep. They can disrupt your internal clock, making it harder to fall asleep at night. However, if you already have a consistent and restful night sleep, there’s no need to worry about daytime rest. Sleep effectiveness is individual, and if you experience difficulty falling asleep at night, consider limiting the timing or shortening the duration of your daytime naps.
  3. Stick to a consistent sleep schedule by going to bed and waking up at the same time each day. Your circadian rhythm, which aligns with sunrise and sunset, supports better sleep quality. Following a regular routine can positively affect the duration and quality of your sleep, reducing the risk of disrupting your circadian rhythm and melatonin levels. If you have trouble falling asleep, try creating a routine with consistent wake-up and bedtime hours — over time, this can become a natural habit that doesn’t even require an alarm.

Relaxation before sleep:

  1. Take a relaxing bath or shower. Scientific studies have confirmed that this can improve sleep quality and help people, especially older adults, fall asleep faster. One study found that taking a hot bath 90 minutes before bed enhances sleep quality and promotes deeper sleep. It’s also worth noting that a cold shower before sleep can contribute to deeper and more restful rest. However, it’s important to remember that extremely hot or cold showers are not recommended for people with heart conditions. Experiment and find the optimal time and type of water therapy that works best for you to support healthy sleep.
  2. Relax and clear your mind in the evening. Try different strategies such as listening to calming music, reading a book, getting a massage, taking a hot bath, meditating, practicing deep breathing, or visualizing peaceful scenes before bed. You can also consider taking a short walk outdoors in the fresh air, which may help you relax and improve your sleep quality. For even faster sleep, try the U.S. Army sleep method: close your eyes and begin the relaxation process. Breathe deeply and slowly, focusing on each inhale and exhale. Pay attention to every part of your body—from head to toe—gradually relaxing each one. Use visualization techniques by imagining yourself in a calm place, or repeat the words “don’t think” for a few seconds to help quiet the mind.
  3. Avoid sleep disruptions. It’s important to mute notifications or enable “Do Not Disturb” mode to prevent unexpected alerts. Remember that smartphones emit electromagnetic waves, which can affect sleep quality. As an alternative, use night mode and consider using wireless headphones to listen to relaxing music or meditation tracks. This can help create a calming atmosphere for falling asleep and improve your overall sleep quality.

Nutrition and Supplements:

  1. Avoid consuming alcohol and caffeine before bedtime. While caffeine can be helpful for boosting focus and energy, having it late in the day can harm your sleep. Research shows that consuming caffeine even 6 hours before sleep can reduce sleep quality. Its effects can last 6 to 8 hours, so it's best to avoid large amounts after 3–4 PM, especially for those sensitive to caffeine or experiencing sleep issues. As for alcohol, it can trigger sleep apnea, disrupt your sleep cycle, and reduce melatonin production. To maintain a healthy circadian rhythm and proper biological processes, it's important to avoid alcohol before bed.
  2. Limit heavy meals before bedtime. Eating late at night can negatively affect sleep quality and the natural release of growth hormone (HGH) and melatonin. It's important to avoid large portions before sleeping, as they may lead to poor sleep and hormonal imbalance. Instead, choosing light and healthy snacks a few hours before bed can help improve sleep quality.
  3. Avoid drinking too much liquid before bedtime, as frequent nighttime urination—known as nocturia—can negatively impact sleep quality and reduce your energy during the day. It’s recommended to limit fluid intake, especially within 1–2 hours before going to bed, to reduce the risk of these symptoms. Additionally, using the bathroom before sleep can help lower the chances of waking up at night and improve overall sleep quality.
  4. Take supplements such as melatonin and others. Melatonin, the key sleep hormone, helps regulate your sleep cycle and signals the brain when it's time to rest. In some countries, melatonin requires a prescription, while in others it’s widely available in stores or online. The recommended dose is typically 1–5 mg taken 30–60 minutes before bedtime. Other effective supplements include glycine (3 g), valerian root (500 mg before bed), magnesium, L-theanine (100–200 mg before bed), and lavender (80–160 mg). These can help enhance relaxation and sleep quality. It’s important to try these supplements one at a time and consider combining them with other natural strategies to support healthy sleep.

Bedroom Organization:

  1. Create a sleep-friendly environment. Many people find that the bedroom’s atmosphere and setup are key factors for a good night’s sleep. To optimize your bedroom conditions, try to reduce outside noise, especially from traffic, control lighting, and avoid artificial light from devices like alarm clocks. Consider using earplugs to further block external sounds and sleep masks to block out light. Make sure your bedroom is quiet, calm, clean, and comfortable.
  2. Maintain an optimal bedroom temperature. Take care to keep the temperature in your bedroom at a comfortable level, as scientific studies show it significantly affects sleep quality. Experiment with different temperatures, but around 20°C (68°F) is considered ideal for most people, promoting calm and restful sleep.
  3. Ensure a comfortable sleeping space. New bedding can improve sleep quality. Additionally, low-quality bedding may worsen back pain. Choosing the best mattress and bedding is very personal. When updating your bedding, follow your own preferences. It is recommended to replace bedding at least every 5–8 years.
